# Summary:
Mewgenics is a turn based tactical combat roguelike, think fire emblem, mixed with light breeding sim / creature training aspects. The gameplay loop consists of taking a party of cats out on an adventure, trying to clear through up to three zones, then returning home with loot for your next adventure and food to breed cats with. Between runs you can try and breed your cats in order to have the next generation of adventurers have better stats or skills passed down from the previous run. The cycle repeats. 


# Gameplay:
The gameplay hits a relatively casual tactical strategy market. Combat contains depth but not an overwhelming amount of complexity, an invested player can find minor optimizations that will add up over a run but the game is not inhibited by an inability to do so. Early game (the first 20 hours) feels plagued by there being a single healer class and it being overwhelmingly more important than everything else.Once you delve more into the breeding, however, and your cats base stats are a little higher you no longer are restricted to a “holy trinity” (tank healer dps) approach to team building. While class balance is somewhat varied, the game succeeds in every class being able to beat the game, some can just do so more consistently than others. There is lots of content to get through, with each in-game act progressing in both complexity of mechanics and difficulty, presenting hundreds of hours of playtime. Personally, I found the events to be underwhelming as they neither offered the player decisions to make nor provided random effects that might make the game feel more exciting. Events in Mewgenics almost always feel like a random event that is likely to hurt you significantly more than it will benefit you, and the negative results feel much more common than the positive. It created an experience where anytime an event was presented to the player it was almost always correct to skip it if possible as the risk was high and the reward was low. I wish they had taken more of the “Slay the Spire” approach where the player is given a strategic decision to make that helps shape the build of the run, or lead it to failure. The more random approach of Mewgenics doesn’t give the player a feeling of agency leading to it being the weakest aspect of the game.


# Packaging:

The game comes packaged with a cute twisted aesthetic layered on top of an edgy 14 year old's sense of humor. The contrast between the look and the primarily toilet and dark humor plays well, although part of what makes it play well is the detail in the execution. It is polished and thought through, never feeling like gags are just there for shock value. The soundtrack really stands out, every major boss has their own theme song filled with silly lyrics without losing any groove or catchiness. This game flourishes based off of its packaging, becoming a standout experience. 


# Price to gametime: 
Price to gametime is always a consideration, there are lots of good 6-12 hour games you can play but sometimes you are left feeling underwhelmed for the price point. Despite Mewgenics’ $40 (CAD) price tag, competing in the roguelike genre that tends to sit in the sub $25 range, it presents a strong game with meaningful depth and metagame development that allows for multiple sessions that don’t feel solved. I think this is often a pit that roguelikes can get stuck in where in looking for a “good run” you are streamlined into specific optimizations that feel repetitive– I found this issue significant in Hades, but in Mewgenics as your cats breed and mutate your criteria for what makes a good run changes. Having put 50 hours into Mewgenics I am sitting at about 40% completion, making it feel worth the higher cost compared to competitors.
